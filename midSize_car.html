Definition

Mid-size cars are also called large family cars in Euro. Mid-size cars have room for five adults and a large trunk (boot). Engines are more powerful than small family/compact cars and some may have engines of more than 2.5 litres. Car sizes were usually around 4.50 m in length by the early 1990s, but are now shifting to 4.80 m or more in length.

First generation V10 (1982-1986)

In 1982 for the 1983 model year, the Camry became an independent model line from Celica Camry, and was sold as a mid-size four-door sedan, five-door hatchback and a more luxurious version of the Camry named Camry Vista sold in the Japanese market. It was available with a 92hp 2.0L I4 engine or a 74hp 2.0L I4 turbodiesel engine, and could be purchased with either a five-speed manual transmission or a four-speed automatic. In contrast to the rear-wheel drive Celica Camry, the Toyota Camry was a front-wheel drive vehicle built on an all-new platform V10. The design of the first-generation Camry fit well within the box-shaped trends of the early 1980s. Additionally, the vehicle size and available options were characteristic of Japanese-designed cars of the time; the Camry was a small, inexpensive sedan with solid but spartan construction and competed indirectly against larger American counterparts.

Second generation V20 (1987-1991)

The second-generation model debuted in 1986 with V20 platform for the 1987 model year, and included a station wagon but dropped the hatchback. In 1988, all-wheel drive (called All-Trac) and a 160hp 2.5L V6 engine, that featured dual overhead camshafts, were added as options for the first time. In 1991, anti-lock brakes became optional on the V6, LE, and wagon models. The 2.5 L engine and Camry chassis was repackaged as the upscale Lexus ES 250. The second-generation Camry was extremely popular in US.

Third generation V30 XV10 (1992-1996)

The Camry V30 was introduced exclusively to the Japanese market in July 1990. The third-generation Camrys had rounded features and a very curved silhouette. A widened version of this model, the Camry XV10 was also produced in 1991, which was designed for international markets. The V30 was mostly identical to the wider XV10 except for the front- and rear-end styling to an otherwise unchanged body. The V30 remained smaller than the XV10 to offer Japanese buyers a sedan that was within Japanese regulations concerning exterior dimensions and engine displacement. After 1991, a four-wheel steering version of the V30 Camry was sold with a 2.0L V6 engine, with the name "Toyota Camry V6 Prominent 4WS".

Fourth generation V40 XV20(1997-2001)

The fourth-generation Camry was launched in Japan in December 1996 based on V40. It continued as a sedan and station wagon. It was launched in the US for the 1997 model year. In 2000, the sedan models received a mid-model upgrade to the front and rear fascias, but remained otherwise similar to the 1999 models. The Lexus ES 300 was again built from the Camry chassis. Power was increased slightly to 133hp for the 5S-FE 2.2 L I4 and 194hp for the 1MZ-FE V6. For the General Export Market, the Camry was offered as 2.2 GLX and 3.0 V6 Grande. This was the first Camry to be sold as a Daihatsu; the Daihatsu Altis was identical to the export version of the Camry. The XV10 replacement, the XV20 Camry, arrived in 1996 as well.

Fifth generation XV30 (2002-2005)

In September 2001, the 2002 model year Toyota Camry was released as a larger sedan, but without a station wagon for the first time. Due to station wagons losing popularity to minivan and crossover SUVs, the Camry wagon was replaced by the Highlander SUV, which utilizes the Camry's platform. Starting from this generation, the Camry had two different distinctive designs, an American version with a more conservative design and an Asian version with more chrome, larger head lamps and tail lamps and a general greater emphasis on its width. Later generation Camry will see greater differentiation, with the Asian Camry adopting a more luxurious design, while the American Camry pursued a more mass-appeal approach.

Sixth generation XV40 (2006-2011)

The XV40 Camry was introduced at the 2006 North American International Auto Show alongside a hybrid version and went on sale in March 2006 for the 2007 model year. This generation of Camry saw even greater differentiation between American/Japanese domestic market Camry and the Asian market Camry. The Asian Camry has a larger body size targeted at a higher end market, priced just below entry-level German luxury models. Between 2006 and 2010, the Daihatsu-badged Altis model sold alongside the Camry in Japan. The Daihatsu differed only in badging, with no cosmetic changes. Power comes from a choice of four and six-cylinder engines. The 2.4L 2AZ-FE I4 engine came with a five-speed manual or five-speed automatic transmission. The 3.5L 2GR-FE V6 in contrast came with a new six-speed automatic. The Camry was facelifted in 2009 for the 2010 model year with a redesigned fascia, taillights, and an all-new 2.5L 2AR-FE four-cylinder engine with a new six-speed automatic transmission. Power locks, stability control, and traction control were also made standard for 2010. The XV40 series Camry is the first in which the Camry has been available as a gasoline/electric hybrid. The Camry Hybrid utilizes Toyota’s second-generation Hybrid Synergy Drive (HSD) and a 2AZ-FXE four-cylinder with 150hp in conjunction with a 40hp electric motor.

Seventh generation XV50 (2011-present)

The new Camry was introduced in August2011. The interior received a major restyling, while the exterior received all-new sheet metal and more angular styling. The Camry line-up for the Japanese domestic market was reduced to being hybrid only. The Japanese market will now share the same Camry model as the Asian market Camry. Power output has been increased mostly by switching to electro-hydraulic power steering. All models are standard with 6-speed automatic transmissions. No manual transmissions are offered. The new model has increased fuel economy due to lighter weight, a sleeker body, and low rolling-resistance tires.

Volkswagen Passat - over 9,200,000

  • Mmanufactured by Volkswagen since 1973
  • The Volkswagen Passat NMS won the 2012 Motor Trend Car of the Year

First generation B1 (Typ 32; 1973-1981)

The original Volkswagen Passat was launched in 1973. The body types offered originally were two- and four-door sedans and similar looking three- and five-door versions. Externally all four shared a modern fastback style design, styled by the Italian designer Giorgetto Giugiaro. In essence, the first Passat was a fastback version of the mechanically identical Audi 80 sedan. A five-door station wagon/estate was introduced in 1974. The Passat was one of the most modern European family cars at the time. The Passat originally used the four-cylinder OHC 1.3L (54hp) and 1.5L(74hp/84hp) petrol engines with either a four-speed manual transmission or three-speed automatic. It had a MacPherson strut front suspension with a solid axle/coil spring setup at the rear. In July 1978 the Passat Diesel became available, equipped with the VW Golf's 1.5L Diesel(49hp), followed in February 1979 by the Passat GLI with a Bosch fuel-injected version of the 1.6L engine. In North America, the car was called the Volkswagen Dasher. A sports version, named Passat TS 1.6 and later Passat GTS 1.8 Pointer was also introduced.

Second generation B2 (Typ 32B; 1981-1988)

The second generation Volkswagen Passat was launched in 1981 based on platform B2. In addition to the Passat hatchbacks and wagon, there was also a conventional three-box saloon, which was sold as the Volkswagen Santana. In North America, the Passat/Santana was sold as the Volkswagen Quantum. The four-wheel drive Syncro version was introduced in October 1984, initially only with the more powerful 2L five-cylinder engine. The Santana was also produced in China and South America. As of 2012 the B2 Santana is still in production in China. An automatic stop/start was also available in some markets. The four-wheel drive system used in the Passat Variant Syncro shared the mechanics of the Audi 80 quattro rather than the Volkswagen Golf Syncro. On March 31, 1988 production ended with 3,345,248 built in Germany. World production totals amount to at least 4.5 million.

Third generation B3 and B4 (Typ 35i; 1988-1996)

The third generation Passat was introduced in March 1988. The lack of a grille made the car's front end styling reminiscent of older, rear-engined Volkswagens and also doubled as a modern styling trend. It was the first transverse-engine layout Passat to be built on a Volkswagen-designed platform, rather than sharing one with an Audi saloon. The car, although designated B3 in Volkswagen's platform nomenclature, was based largely on the A platform as used for the smaller Golf model, but was stretched. Only 4-door saloon and 5-door estate versions were available. The 5-door hatchback version is no longer available. It was marketed under the Passat name in all markets. The new VR6 2.8L engine gave the top-of-the-range Passat a top speed of 224kmph.

The B3 Passat was heavily facelifted in 1993 and although it was designated B4, it was not an all-new model. The facelift revised external body panels with most obvious exterior change seeing the re-introduction of a grille. The interior was mildly updated and included safety equipment such as dual front airbags and seat belt pre-tensioners, although the basic dashboard design remained unchanged. The car was available with a Turbocharged Direct Injection (TDI) diesel engine - an inline four-cylinder 1.9 L turbodiesel, generating 210NM/1,900 rpm, 89 bhp/3,750 rpm.

Fourth generation B5 and B5.5 (Typ 3B/3BG; 1996-2005)

An all-new Passat, based on the B5 platform, was launched in 1996. It was shared with the first-generation Audi A4, which was successor to the Audi 80 line. The Passat sharing its longitundinal engine layout platform with Audi's equivalent model for the first time since Passat B2. B5 introduced a new design language, for the latest generation of Volkswagens such as the Mk4 Golf, Bora and Polo Mk4. Aerodynamic work gave the B5 Passat a coefficient of drag of 0.27 (saloon model). The car featured a fully independent four-link front suspension and a semi-independent rear suspension. The 4motion 4WD models was introduced in 1997 with a fully independent suspension. Three transmission options were available: a 5-speed manual transmission, a 6-speed MT and a 5-speed AT with Tiptronic. A lengthened platform was introduced in China in late 1999. This long-wheelbase version was rebadged and launched in Europe as the Škoda Superb in 2001. An updated version called the Passat Lingyu was released in 2005. This receives the 2L, 1.8L Turbo, and 2.8L V6 petrol engines. At the 2009 Shanghai Motor Show, an updated Passat Lingyu was shown.

B5 Passat models built after late 2000, also known as B5.5 models, received minor styling and mechanical revisions. A 4L W8 engine was introduced in 2001 in a luxury version of the car that included standard 4motion. This engine was intended to be a test bed for Volkswagen Group's new W engine technology, which would later make an appearance on the W12 in the Phaeton and Audi A8, and the W16 engine in the Bugatti Veyron. The engine was discontinued in 2004.

Fifth generation B6 B7 NMS (Typ 3C; 2005-present)

The B6 Passat was first displayed in March 2005. It no longer shared its platform with Audi A4. Based on a modified version of the Mk5 Golf's PQ46 platform, the B6 featured a transverse rather than longitundinal engine layout of its predecessor. FSI(Fuel Stratified Injection) is used in nearly every petrol engined version of the Passat, ranging from 1.6L to 3.6L. In February 2008, the 2.0 FSI was replaced with the new Audi-developed 1.8 TSI engine and 6-speed automatic transmission. The 1.8T is 158 hp, 250NM and reaches 0–100 km/h in 8.6 seconds, reaching a top speed of 220km/h. In Asia, the PQ46 Passat was released by FAW-VW as the Magotan, after Shanghai Volkswagen had decided to continue using the B5 platform for the Passat and the Passat Lingyu. Exclusive to Europe was a limited edition Passat R36 with a 3.6L V6 engine, that putting out 300 hp, and All Wheel Drive.

The 4-door coupe version CC (Comfort Coupe) of the Passat debuted in 2008. The Passat CC intends to be more stylish and luxurious than the previously released Passat B6. Some options specific to the CC include hands-free parking, lane-departure prevention, intelligent cruise control, and adaptive suspension. Engines offered in the CC mirror those of the regular Passat.

The B6 Passat was facelifted and shown in September 2010. Although designated "B7", the car is not an all-new model. The facelift resulted in new external body panels. Overall height and width dimensions are unchanged from the B6 Passat. The interior featured minor detail changes from the B6, although the basic dashboard design remain unchanged. In China, the FAW-VW-built Magotan was replaced by a long-wheelbase version of the Passat B7. The new model will be exclusive to the Chinese market, and at least 100 mm longer than the European B7. Engines available for Magotan B7L are ranging from 1.4T, 1.8T to 2.0T, a special-developed EA390 3.0L VR6 FSI engine is opted for the top model.

Known as the New Midsize Sedan (NMS) before its unveiling in January 2011, this unique larger model designed specifically for the North American market replaces the B6 Passat sedan and wagon for the 2012 model year. It is also built and sold in China alongside the Passat Lingyu and Magotan.

Honda Civic - over 16,500,000

  • Having gone through several generational changes, the Civic has become larger and more upmarket
  • Initially gaining a reputation for being fuel-efficient, reliable, and environmentally friendly
  • Later iterations have become known for performance and sportiness
  • Third in the European Car of the Year awards, the highest ranking for a Japanese vehicle in 1973
  • Car of the Year in 1974
  • On Car and Driver magazine's yearly Ten Best list six times, in 1985, 1988-91, and 1996
  • International Car of the Year in 2005
  • North American Car of the Year in 2006

First generation (1972-1979) SB1, SG, SE, VB

Introduced in 1972, but sold as a 1973 model. The Civic was available as a coupe, both a three- and a five-door hatchback, as well as a five-door station wagon. Due to the 1973 oil crisis, consumer demand for fuel efficient vehicles was high, Civic succeeded with CVCC engine debuted in 1975, which allowed for more efficient combustion and being able to run on either leaded or unleaded fuel. The Civic was joined by a platform expansion of the three-door hatchback, called the Honda Accord in 1976.

Second generation (1979-1983) SL, SS, SR, ST, VC, WD

Introduced in 1979 as a 1980 model. It was larger, had a more angular shape, and came with increased engine power. All Civic engines now used the CVCC design, which added a 3rd valve per cylinder. Three transmissions were offered: a four-speed manual, a five-speed manual, and a two-speed semi-automatic. A four door version called the Ballade was built, under agreement, by Mercedes Benz South Africa.

Third generation (1983-1987) AG, AH, AJ, AK, AT, EC

Released in 1983 for the 1984 model year with new long running four-cylinder D series CVCC engine. An additional two-seat coupe style labeled CRX was introduced. 1984 also saw the high-performance SI model for the Japanese market, featuring upgraded suspension and the 1.6L DOHC engine which was rated at 130ps. SI models were offered in the US as a 3-door Civic SI hatchback and the CRX SI variant with a 91ps fuel-injected SOHC 12-valve engine. Some models equiped with 4WD system and manual transmission featured a synchronized 6th gear.

Fourth generation (1987-1991) - EC, ED, EE, EF, SH

In 1987, the Civic was redesigned with increased dimensions and a lower hood line. A wide range of models were offered for various markets around the world. The most notable of which was the Japanese market SIR (featuring the B16A DOHC VTEC engine with 160hp). All U.S. models featured electronic fuel injection. The fourth generation saw the introduction of a fully independent rear suspension across the entire model range.

Fifth generation (1992-1995) - EG, EH, EJ1, EJ2

Introduced in 1991, the redesigned Civic featured increased dimensions, as well as more aerodynamic styling. The redesigned Civic featured increased dimensions, as well as more aerodynamic styling. The fifth generation remains popular among tuners and racers alike, continuing in the sporty tradition of the original Civic SIR, equiped with the excellent double wishbone front suspension design.

Sixth generation (1996-2000) - EK3, EK4, EK5, EK9, EJ6, EJ7, EJ8, EJ9, EM1

The sixth generation featured updated styling although less radical than previous redesigns. CVT gearbox firstly equiped. all base models were made with 1.6L engines. All base models were made with 1.6L engines (Honda D-series engine). In 1998, the Civic SIR was transformed into Civic Type R B16B engine producing 185ps to achieve Honda's idea of a car capable of high performance on the circuit.

Seventh generation (2001-2005) - EM2, ES1, EP1, EP2, EP3, EP4, EU1, EV1

The seventh generation was released in September 2000. While the redesign retained the previous generation's exterior dimensions, interior space was improved in part by using a flat rear floor. The front suspension was changed from that of a double wishbone to a MacPherson strut, in order to lower costs, as well as allow more engine bay room for the newly introduced Honda K-series engine. The Type R was redesigned as well using a more powerful i-VTEC motor (K20A2, 200ps) and using the three-door hatchback body style. This generation saw Honda introduce their first Civic Hybrid, powered by a 1.3-litre engine.

Eighth generation (2006-2011) - FD2, FD7, FA1, FG1, FG2, FA5, FK, FN, MK8

For the eighth generation, Honda split the model into two different platforms, one for sedan and coupe, and one for a hatchback designed primarily for the European market using a simpler rear suspension from the Honda Fit and more aggressive styling. The end of this model generation also marked the end of the Civic Type-R.

Toyota Corolla - over 40,000,000

  • Mmanufactured by Toyota since 1966
  • Has been one of the best selling cars in the world since 1974
  • In 1997, became the best selling nameplate in the world, surpassing the Volkswagen Beetle
  • Toyota reached the milestone of 40 million Corollas sold over eleven generations in July 2013

First generation (E10) (1966-1969)

Introduced in October 1966 with the new 1100 cc K pushrod engine including a 2-door sedan, 2-door coupe called Corolla Sprinter, 4-door sedan, and 3-door wagon. In 1970, the Toyota Corolla became the number 2 best selling import in the US.

Second generation (E20) (1970-1973)

Appeared in 1970 for the 1971 model year with more rounded body and the 1400 cc T and 1600 cc 2T pushrod engines. It had a longer wheelbase. The front suspension design was improved greatly, using a swaybar, however the rear remained relatively the same. A 5-door wagon was added. The Corolla Levin and Sprinter Trueno names were introduced as the enhanced performance version of the Corolla and Sprinter respectively when a DOHC version of the 2T engine was introduced in March 1972

Third generation (E30, E40, E50, E60) (1974-1978)

The third-generation Toyota Corolla built from 1974-81 worldwide versions, marked Toyota's greatest growth in the United States. Producing more than 300,000 every year, began to be the best selling cars in the world and marked Japan as the major exporter of cars

Fourth generation (E70) (1979-1982)

Released in 1979 with a square edged design. It was the last generation to have the enitire lineup in rear wheel drive and last generation to use the K and T series engines. The new A series engines were added to the range as a running change. Fuel injection was introduced as an extra cost. By the time of 1983, over 10 million was built world wide in the shortest period of 16 years and 4 months.

Fifth generation (E80) (1983-1986)

The fifth generation is generally regarded as the most popular Corolla. This model, from 1984, moved the Corolla into front wheel drive except the AE85 and AE86, which were to be the last Corollas offered in FR layout and with a 1.6L producing an impressive 124hp. The AE85 shares its chassis and basic design with the famous AE86, but while the AE86 was designed for performance, the AE85 was designed for economy and differs mainly in its engine. They were sold as Toyota Corolla Levin (with the fixed headlights) and Toyota Sprinter Trueno (with pop-up lights). From 1985, re-badged E80 Sprinters were sold in the U.S. as the fifth generation Chevrolet Nova. Also in 1987, the first US-assembled Corolla rolled off the assembly line in Fremont, California.

Sixth generation (E90) (1987-1990)

Introduced in 1987 with more rounded and aerodynamic style. Overall this generation has a more refined feel than older Corollas and other older subcompacts. Most models were now front wheel drive, along with a few 4WD All-Trac models. Many engines were used on a wide array of trim levels and models, ranging from the 1.3L 2E to the 165hp supercharged DOHC 4A-GZE. The Sprinter was also rebadged and sold as the Geo Prizm (US) or Holden Nova (Australia).

Seventh generation (E100) (1991-1994)

In June 1991, the seventh generation Corolla was introduced for the 1993 model year. It was larger, heavier, and more expensive, have the completely rounded, aerodynamic shape of the 1990s, with development chief Dr Akihiko Saito wanting to develop a 'mini-Lexus'. In 1997, the 20 millionth Corolla rolled out from the factory to the showroom.

Eighth generation (E110) (1995-1999)

Production of the E110 Corolla started in May 1995. The model range began to change as Toyota decided styling differences would improve sales in different markets. In 1998, for the first time, some non-Japanese Corollas received the new 1ZZ-FE engine, which was the first in Toyota to have an aluminum engine block and aluminum cylinder heads, making this generation lighter than the E100 Corolla. VVT-i variable valve timing was added to the engine for 2000.

Ninth generation (E120, E130) (2000-2005)

In November 2000, the ninth-generation Corolla was introduced in Japan, with edgier styling and more technology to bring the nameplate into the 21st century. The car was powered by a 1.8L DOHC, 16-valve engine rated at 130hp. The facelifted Corolla since then is called Corolla Altis in the ASEAN region. The station wagon model is called the Corolla Fielder. For the 2005 model year, a sporty XRS model was introduced. It was powered by a high-revving 172hp 2ZZ-GE engine and 6-Speed MT. The Corolla has also spawned another multi-purpose vehicle, the Toyota Matrix, sold in Canada and the United US. In 2005, the 30 millionth Corolla rolled out from the factory to the showroom.

Tenth generation (E140, E150) (2006-)

Launched in October 2006 with greatly increased size and VVT-i engine. Japanese markets called it the Corolla Axio, with the ASEAN markets retaining the Corolla Altis branding. The station wagon retains the Corolla Fielder name. The Corolla Altis and Corolla Axio have a different appearance and size. 5-star in safety index given by NCAP, making it one of Japan's automakers in the safest small cars.

Ninth generation (E120, E130) (2000-2007)

In November 2000, the ninth-generation Corolla was introduced in Japan, with edgier styling and more technology to bring the nameplate into the 21st century. The car was powered by a 1.8L DOHC, 16-valve engine rated at 130hp. For the 2005 model year, a sporty XRS model was introduced. It was powered by a high-revving 172hp 2ZZ-GE engine and 6-Speed MT. The Corolla has also spawned another multi-purpose vehicle, the Toyota Matrix, sold in Canada and the United US. In 2005, the 30 millionth Corolla rolled out from the factory to the showroom.
