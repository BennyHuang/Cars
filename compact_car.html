Definition

Compact cars also called small family cars in Euro. Compact cars have room for five adults and usually have engines between 1.4 and 2.2 litres, but some have engines of up to 2.5 litres. Current compact car size is approximately 4,100 mm to 4,450 mm long for hatchbacks, or 4,400 mm to 4,750 mm long for convertibles, sedans or station wagons. Multi-purpose vehicles and sport utility vehicles based on compact cars (often called compact MPVs and compact SUVs) have similar sizes, ranging from 4,400 mm to 4,700 mm . These are the most popular vehicles in most developed countries.

Volkswagen Golf - over 30,000,000

  • Mmanufactured by Volkswagen since 1974 to replace for Volkswagen Beetle
  • Marketed worldwide across seven generations in various body configurations
  • Volkswagen's best-selling model and the world's second best-selling model, with more than 30 million built by mid June 2013
  • World Car of the Year in 2009 with the Volkswagen Golf Mk6 and in 2013 with the Volkswagen Golf Mk7
  • Every generation of Golf has been a runner-up in the European Car of the Year awards and two have been winners, the Golf Mk3 in 1992 and the Golf Mk7 in 2013.
  • The Volkswagen Golf has made the Car and Driver annual 10Best list multiple times

First generation (A1/Typ 17, 1974-1983)

In May 1974, Volkswagen presented the first-generation Golf as a modern front-wheel-drive, long-range replacement for the Volkswagen Beetle. Later Golf variations included the Golf GTI "hot hatch" (introduced in June 1976), a diesel-powered version (from September 1976), the Jetta notchback saloon version (from October 1979), the Volkswagen Golf Cabriolet (from January 1980) and a Golf-based pickup, the Volkswagen Caddy.

Second generation (A2/Typ 19E/1G, 1983-1992)

Introduced in 1983, it grew slightly in terms of wheelbase, exterior and interior dimensions. In 1985, the first Golfs with four-wheel-drive Golf syncro went on sale, exclusively released on the continent in 1989 with 120 kW (160 bhp) and ABS braking. There was no Mk2-based cabriolet model; instead, the Mk1 Cabriolet was continued over the Mk2's entire production run.

Third generation (A3/Typ 1H, 1993-2001)

It again grew slightly in comparison with its immediate predecessor, while its wheelbase remained unchanged. New engines included the first TDI diesel engine in a Golf, and a narrow-angle 2.8 L VR6 engine. Golf Variant joined the line-up in September 1993. At the same time, a completely new Mk3-derived Cabriolet was introduced, replacing the 13-year-old Mk1-based version. The notchback version Jetta III was presented in 1992 January. It was European Car of the Year for 1992.

Fourth generation (A4/Typ 1J, 1997-2006)

First introduced in August 1997 based on PQ34 platform, followed by a notchback version VW Bora or VW Jetta in August 1998 and a new Golf Variant in March 1999. New high-performance models included the 3.2 L VR6-engined four-wheel-drive Golf R32 introduced in 2002, as well as use of the famous 1.8T (turbo) 4-cylinder used in various Volkswagen Group models.

Fifth generation (A5/Typ 1K, 2004.5-2008)

Introduced in Europe in 2003 based on PQ35 platform. A GTI version is powered by a turbocharged version of the 2.0 TFSI engine, producing 147kW. The new model called GLI, with the only difference being that the GLI is a sedan, while the GTI is a hatchback.

Sixth generation (A6/Typ 5K, 2008-2013)

Debuted in 2008 on the existing PQ35 platform. The design is said to be more aerodynamic, more cheeper, helping fuel efficiency and is quieter than its predecessor. New on the Golf is the optional Volkswagen Adaptive Chassis Control which allows the driver to select between 'normal', 'comfort' and 'sports' modes. The Mk6 Golf is available with 6- or 7-speed DSG. The GTI version is equipped with a 200-hp, turbocharged TSI gas engine while the Golf R has a 256-hp TFSI engine. The Volkswagen Golf Mk6 is a 2012 IIHS Top Safety Pick

Seventh generation (A7/Typ 5G, 2013-present)

The Golf VII using the new MQB platform, debuted in late 2012 at the Paris Motor Show. It is slightly larger than the Mk6 while managing to be approximately 100 kg lighter, depending on engine choice. The GTI will offer a 217 bhp turbocharged 2.0-litre four-cylinder with an available performance pack to raise the output to 227 bhp.

First generation (1998-2005)

Introduced by Ford of Europe in 1998 to the European market as a replacement for the Ford Escort and began marketing for North America in October 1999. The car launched as a 3-door hatchback, 4-door sedan and 5-door wagon; a 5-door hatchback debuted in 2000. Equiped with class-leading, independent multi-link rear suspension, marketed as Control Blade suspension

, while many competitors used the less expensive non-independent suspension.

Second generation (2005-2011)

First launched based on C1 platform in September 2004. The basic suspension design was carried over largely unchanged from its predecessor Mk1. Though the same body styles as the Mk1 were offered, Mk 2 is larger and considerably heavier, as a result the interior and boot space have increased. New technologies include a KeyFree system, a solar-reflect windshield, adaptive front lighting, Bluetooth hands-free phones and voice control for audio, telephone and climate control systems. For the North American market, development followed a separate path. since debuting in 2007. In 2005, Ford released a version of Ford's sports division of Focus, the Focus ST. This one produced 225 bhp, over 50 bhp more than the MK.I ST, and could achieve a 0-60 mph time of just 6.4 seconds, and a 152 mph top speed.

Third generation (2011-present)

In 2010 Ford decided to reunite both international and North American models by releasing the international Mk3 worldwide and it was unveiled in 2011, debuting a new 2.0L direct injection I4 engine. This new generation of Focus incorporates a redesigned cabin with improved materials and new entertainment technologies.

  • Initially gaining a reputation for being fuel-efficient, reliable, and environmentally friendly
  • Later iterations have become known for performance and sportiness
  • Third in the European Car of the Year awards, the highest ranking for a Japanese vehicle in 1973
  • Car of the Year in 1974
  • On Car and Driver magazine's yearly Ten Best list six times, in 1985, 1988-91, and 1996
  • International Car of the Year in 2005

First generation (1972-1979) SB1, SG, SE, VB

Introduced in 1972, but sold as a 1973 model. The Civic was available as a coupe, both a three- and a five-door hatchback, as well as a five-door station wagon. Due to the 1973 oil crisis, consumer demand for fuel efficient vehicles was high, Civic succeeded with CVCC engine debuted in 1975, which allowed for more efficient combustion and being able to run on either leaded or unleaded fuel. The Civic was joined by a platform expansion of the three-door hatchback, called the Honda Accord in 1976.

Second generation (1979-1983) SL, SS, SR, ST, VC, WD

Introduced in 1979 as a 1980 model. It was larger, had a more angular shape, and came with increased engine power. All Civic engines now used the CVCC design, which added a 3rd valve per cylinder. Three transmissions were offered: a four-speed manual, a five-speed manual, and a two-speed semi-automatic. A four door version called the Ballade was built, under agreement, by Mercedes Benz South Africa.

Third generation (1983-1987) AG, AH, AJ, AK, AT, EC

Released in 1983 for the 1984 model year with new long running four-cylinder D series CVCC engine. An additional two-seat coupe style labeled CRX was introduced. 1984 also saw the high-performance SI model for the Japanese market, featuring upgraded suspension and the 1.6L DOHC engine which was rated at 130ps. SI models were offered in the US as a 3-door Civic SI hatchback and the CRX SI variant with a 91ps fuel-injected SOHC 12-valve engine. Some models equiped with 4WD system and manual transmission featured a synchronized 6th gear.

Fourth generation (1987-1991) - EC, ED, EE, EF, SH

In 1987, the Civic was redesigned with increased dimensions and a lower hood line. A wide range of models were offered for various markets around the world. The most notable of which was the Japanese market SIR (featuring the B16A DOHC VTEC engine with 160hp). All U.S. models featured electronic fuel injection. The fourth generation saw the introduction of a fully independent rear suspension across the entire model range.

Fifth generation (1992-1995) - EG, EH, EJ1, EJ2

Introduced in 1991, the redesigned Civic featured increased dimensions, as well as more aerodynamic styling. The redesigned Civic featured increased dimensions, as well as more aerodynamic styling. The fifth generation remains popular among tuners and racers alike, continuing in the sporty tradition of the original Civic SIR, equiped with the excellent double wishbone front suspension design.

Sixth generation (1996-2000) - EK3, EK4, EK5, EK9, EJ6, EJ7, EJ8, EJ9, EM1

The sixth generation featured updated styling although less radical than previous redesigns. CVT gearbox firstly equiped. all base models were made with 1.6L engines. All base models were made with 1.6L engines (Honda D-series engine). In 1998, the Civic SIR was transformed into Civic Type R B16B engine producing 185ps to achieve Honda's idea of a car capable of high performance on the circuit.

Seventh generation (2001-2005) - EM2, ES1, EP1, EP2, EP3, EP4, EU1, EV1

The seventh generation was released in September 2000. While the redesign retained the previous generation's exterior dimensions, interior space was improved in part by using a flat rear floor. The front suspension was changed from that of a double wishbone to a MacPherson strut, in order to lower costs, as well as allow more engine bay room for the newly introduced Honda K-series engine. The Type R was redesigned as well using a more powerful i-VTEC motor (K20A2, 200ps) and using the three-door hatchback body style. This generation saw Honda introduce their first Civic Hybrid, powered by a 1.3-litre engine.

Eighth generation (2006-2011) - FD2, FD7, FA1, FG1, FG2, FA5, FK, FN, MK8

For the eighth generation, Honda split the model into two different platforms, one for sedan and coupe, and one for a hatchback designed primarily for the European market using a simpler rear suspension from the Honda Fit and more aggressive styling. The end of this model generation also marked the end of the Civic Type-R.

Toyota Corolla - over 40,000,000

  • Mmanufactured by Toyota since 1966
  • Has been one of the best selling cars in the world since 1974
  • In 1997, became the best selling nameplate in the world, surpassing the Volkswagen Beetle
  • Toyota reached the milestone of 40 million Corollas sold over eleven generations in July 2013

First generation (E10) (1966-1969)

Introduced in October 1966 with the new 1100 cc K pushrod engine including a 2-door sedan, 2-door coupe called Corolla Sprinter, 4-door sedan, and 3-door wagon. In 1970, the Toyota Corolla became the number 2 best selling import in the US.

Second generation (E20) (1970-1973)

Appeared in 1970 for the 1971 model year with more rounded body and the 1400 cc T and 1600 cc 2T pushrod engines. It had a longer wheelbase. The front suspension design was improved greatly, using a swaybar, however the rear remained relatively the same. A 5-door wagon was added. The Corolla Levin and Sprinter Trueno names were introduced as the enhanced performance version of the Corolla and Sprinter respectively when a DOHC version of the 2T engine was introduced in March 1972

Third generation (E30, E40, E50, E60) (1974-1978)

The third-generation Toyota Corolla built from 1974-81 worldwide versions, marked Toyota's greatest growth in the United States. Producing more than 300,000 every year, began to be the best selling cars in the world and marked Japan as the major exporter of cars

Fourth generation (E70) (1979-1982)

Released in 1979 with a square edged design. It was the last generation to have the enitire lineup in rear wheel drive and last generation to use the K and T series engines. The new A series engines were added to the range as a running change. Fuel injection was introduced as an extra cost. By the time of 1983, over 10 million was built world wide in the shortest period of 16 years and 4 months.

Fifth generation (E80) (1983-1986)

The fifth generation is generally regarded as the most popular Corolla. This model, from 1984, moved the Corolla into front wheel drive except the AE85 and AE86, which were to be the last Corollas offered in FR layout and with a 1.6L producing an impressive 124hp. The AE85 shares its chassis and basic design with the famous AE86, but while the AE86 was designed for performance, the AE85 was designed for economy and differs mainly in its engine. They were sold as Toyota Corolla Levin (with the fixed headlights) and Toyota Sprinter Trueno (with pop-up lights). From 1985, re-badged E80 Sprinters were sold in the U.S. as the fifth generation Chevrolet Nova. Also in 1987, the first US-assembled Corolla rolled off the assembly line in Fremont, California.

Sixth generation (E90) (1987-1990)

Introduced in 1987 with more rounded and aerodynamic style. Overall this generation has a more refined feel than older Corollas and other older subcompacts. Most models were now front wheel drive, along with a few 4WD All-Trac models. Many engines were used on a wide array of trim levels and models, ranging from the 1.3L 2E to the 165hp supercharged DOHC 4A-GZE. The Sprinter was also rebadged and sold as the Geo Prizm (US) or Holden Nova (Australia).

Seventh generation (E100) (1991-1994)

In June 1991, the seventh generation Corolla was introduced for the 1993 model year. It was larger, heavier, and more expensive, have the completely rounded, aerodynamic shape of the 1990s, with development chief Dr Akihiko Saito wanting to develop a 'mini-Lexus'. In 1997, the 20 millionth Corolla rolled out from the factory to the showroom.

Eighth generation (E110) (1995-1999)

Production of the E110 Corolla started in May 1995. The model range began to change as Toyota decided styling differences would improve sales in different markets. In 1998, for the first time, some non-Japanese Corollas received the new 1ZZ-FE engine, which was the first in Toyota to have an aluminum engine block and aluminum cylinder heads, making this generation lighter than the E100 Corolla. VVT-i variable valve timing was added to the engine for 2000.

Ninth generation (E120, E130) (2000-2005)

In November 2000, the ninth-generation Corolla was introduced in Japan, with edgier styling and more technology to bring the nameplate into the 21st century. The car was powered by a 1.8L DOHC, 16-valve engine rated at 130hp. The facelifted Corolla since then is called Corolla Altis in the ASEAN region. The station wagon model is called the Corolla Fielder. For the 2005 model year, a sporty XRS model was introduced. It was powered by a high-revving 172hp 2ZZ-GE engine and 6-Speed MT. The Corolla has also spawned another multi-purpose vehicle, the Toyota Matrix, sold in Canada and the United US. In 2005, the 30 millionth Corolla rolled out from the factory to the showroom.

Tenth generation (E140, E150) (2006-)

Launched in October 2006 with greatly increased size and VVT-i engine. Japanese markets called it the Corolla Axio, with the ASEAN markets retaining the Corolla Altis branding. The station wagon retains the Corolla Fielder name. The Corolla Altis and Corolla Axio have a different appearance and size. 5-star in safety index given by NCAP, making it one of Japan's automakers in the safest small cars.

Ninth generation (E120, E130) (2000-2007)

In November 2000, the ninth-generation Corolla was introduced in Japan, with edgier styling and more technology to bring the nameplate into the 21st century. The car was powered by a 1.8L DOHC, 16-valve engine rated at 130hp. For the 2005 model year, a sporty XRS model was introduced. It was powered by a high-revving 172hp 2ZZ-GE engine and 6-Speed MT. The Corolla has also spawned another multi-purpose vehicle, the Toyota Matrix, sold in Canada and the United US. In 2005, the 30 millionth Corolla rolled out from the factory to the showroom.
